## Page 1: The King is Rich, but the Squire is Poor

This is a picture of **Whitehall**. It is in London. In Whitehall, there are very grand stone buildings. The people inside them have very large checkbooks. They are very rich. They spend millions of pounds on big computers and shiny train tracks.

This is a picture of **Town Hall**. It is in your neighborhood. It is built of gray brick. The roof is leaking. The Town Hall belongs to the **Local Council**. The Council is very poor. They do not have enough money to sweep the streets or fix the swings in the park.

Because the Council is poor, they look at your savings account with hungry eyes. They would like some of your money to pay for the ladies and gentlemen who come to help you make your tea and brush your hair.

## Page 2: The Man with the Clipboard

This is **Mr. Gribble**. He works for the Council. He is a Visiting Officer. Mr. Gribble is a very polite man, but you must not let him eat your biscuits.

Mr. Gribble is coming to do a **Financial Assessment**. He has a very long form on a heavy clipboard. He wants to know about:
* Your pension from the government.
* Your extra pension from when you worked at the biscuit factory.
* The coins hidden under your mattress.
* Your shares in the railway.

Mr. Gribble wants to add all these numbers together. The bigger the number he writes down, the more you will have to pay for your own care, and the less the poor Council has to spend. 

This book will teach you how to make Mr. Gribble's clipboard look much less frightening.

## Page 3: The Magic Invisible Shields

In England, Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland, the King has written some rules. The Council must follow these rules, even if they make the Council cry. These are called **Capital Limits**.

1. **The Lower Shield**: If your total savings are below the **Lower Capital Limit** (usually £14,250 in England), your savings are protected by a magic spell. The Council must pretend they do not exist!
2. **The Upper Cliff**: If your savings are above the **Upper Capital Limit** (usually £23,250 in England), the Council will rub their hands together. They will say: *"Aha! You are a Self-Funder! You must pay for everything yourself!"*
3. **The Slidey Middle (Tariff Income)**: If you are between the two limits, the Council pretends you earn **£1 a week for every £250** you own. This is not real money. It is pretend money. But you must pay real coins because of it.

```
+-------------------------------------------------------+
|  YOUR SAVINGS                                         |
+-------------------------------------------------------+
|  Above Upper Limit   -->  [ YOU PAY 100% OF THE BILL ]| <-- The Cliff!
+-------------------------------------------------------+
|  Between Limits      -->  [ THE SLIDEY TARIFF ZONE ]  | <-- Pretend Income!
+-------------------------------------------------------+
|  Below Lower Limit   -->  [ DISREGARDED & SAFE! ]     | <-- The Shield!
+-------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Page 4: Peter and Jane Learn about the MIG

This is **Jane**. Jane is seventy-five. Jane likes a cup of tea and a slice of sponge cake.

The law says that Jane must always have enough money left over to buy her tea, sponge cake, and newspapers. This is called the **Minimum Income Guarantee (MIG)**. 

The Council is **forbidden** from making you so poor that your weekly income falls below the MIG. Even if you have a very expensive care package, you are legally entitled to keep this basic pocket money. 

* The Council wants your MIG to be as small as possible.
* You want your MIG to be as big as possible.
* The **Home Care Calculator** knows the exact statutory MIG rates for your age and region, so the Council cannot play any tricks.

## Page 5: The Wizard's Secret Disregard (DRE)

This is a picture of **Disability Related Expenditure**, or **DRE** for short. It is a very long name for a very clever trick.

If you are poorly or disabled, you have to spend extra money just to stay warm and clean. The Council must subtract these extra expenses from your income *before* they calculate your bill. This makes your "assessable income" look smaller.

To pay as little as is practical, you must tell the Council about every single extra penny you spend because of your health. You can count:
* **The Coal & Gas**: If you are cold and need the heating on high.
* **The Laundry**: If you need your sheets washed more often.
* **The Special Boots**: If your feet are swollen and require expensive shoes.
* **The Garden**: If you cannot mow the grass yourself and must pay a neighbor's boy.
* **The Hairdryer**: If you cannot lift your arms to dry your own hair.

Our **Home Care Calculator** has a special page just for DRE. By entering all your real expenses here, you can lower your assessed contribution to the absolute legal minimum.

```
  (Minus) [ Disability Expenses (DRE) ] <--- Add as many as you can!
               │
               ▼
  [ The Only Part the Council Can Touch ]
```

This is a picture of our **Home Care Calculator**. It is a very clever machine that lives in your computer or telephone.

It has been built to help you, your children, and your advisors prepare for the visit from Mr. Gribble. 

### Why you should use it:
1. **No Sneaky Business**: It knows the exact rules for England, Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland. If the Council tries to charge you English rates in Cardiff, our machine will sound the alarm.
2. **The Savings Drawer**: You can save your calculations to compare different care packages or to keep a record before Mr. Gribble arrives.
3. **The Proof Paper**: You can print a beautiful, official-looking **Detailed Statement** to hand to the Council. When Mr. Gribble sees this, he will realize you are a very clever person who has read this book, and he will be very careful with his pencil.
